A Vibration, Infinitesimal and Immense

I assumed it would happen eventually, but am surprised that it happened this decade: The gravitational wave detector and observation team won the 2017 Nobel Prize for Physics.

By Andrew Cheeseman, as published on the A Vibration blog. (Note: Although I usually develop tech companies, divisions, and products, I find astrophysics fascinating. So I wrote a thing.)

2017 Nobel Physics Prize winners Rainer Weiss, Barry Barish, & Kip Thorne

Long-Term Planning for Your Nobel Prize

Let's say you want to make a difference, and then you’d like to win the Noble physics prize for your work. The time between your groundbreaking physics discovery and the day you get the call that you’ve won the Nobel physics prize has been averaging about 30 years. So get crackin’ because you might have to wait a while. And even though the prizes are increasingly awarded to 2 or 3 individuals at a time (3 is the maximum), the prize has been awarded only 111 times in the 116 years since the first Nobel physics prize was awarded to Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen (the Royal Swedish Academy took some years off around World War II). So your odds are low to begin with.

I am therefore amazed and also pleasantly surprised with the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences for moving so quickly in awarding the Nobel prize for physics to Rainer Weiss, Kip Thorne, and Barry Barish for their “decisive contributions to the LIGO detector and the observation of gravitational waves,” although we know that the number of scientists and organizations who made decisive contributions is in the thousands [see the list of LIGO authors below].

Record Time?

The discovery and measurement of actual gravitational waves was made in 2015, and it took only two years for Weiss, Thorne, and Barish to win their prizes. That is, “only two years” if you look past the fact that Joseph Weber pioneered the effort of a super-sensitive interferometer for measuring gravitational waves in the 1960’s, that two scientists proposed the idea in 1962 n Russia, and that the concept was first demonstrated in 1962 by Rainer Weiss, that increasingly sophisticated prototype detectors were built in the late 60’s & 70’s; and if you look past the fact that Kip Thorne created a Gravitational Wave Caltech research group 1968, and kept working on it for 49 years; and that an experimental gravitational research group was then created in the late 1970’s, that the US NSF funded a prototype in 1980, that Caltech and MIT agreed to jointly design & build LIGO in 1984, that the National Science Board approved (and Congress appropriated funds for) LIGO’s first year of finding in 1991. So it’s not exactly an overnight sensation (it’s around 50 years), except in astronomical timeframes, where it definitely is overnight or even likety-split.

Heroes

I also want to offer (for what it’s worth) bear hugs and huge kudos to the institutions that support this important research about the nature of our universe: the universities like California Institute of Technology in Pasadena, Massachusetts Institute of Technology in Cambridge, and the University of Glasgow; the foundations like the U.S. National Science Foundation, the UK Science and Technology Facilities Council, the Max Planck Society of Germany, and the Australian Research Council; and the 40 other institutions that support this work (see the Appendix-y section below). On behalf of humanity, thank you for investing in basic science research!!

Future Observatories

I am excited that in addition to the 2 observatories that detected the most recent waves (the LIGO Livingston Observatory in Livingston Louisiana, and the LIGO Hanford Observatory on the DOE Hanford Site near Richland Washington), a new LIGO detector just came online in Europe: the Virgo detector near Pisa, Italy (a collaboration between Italy, France, Netherlands, Poland, Hugary, and Spain). It already detected gravitational waves. We expect future observatories to be INDIGO (LIGO-India), which is a joint project of the Institute of Plasma Research (IPR), Inter-University Centre for Astronomy and Astrophysics (IUCAA), and Raja Ramanna Centre for Advanced Technology (RRCAT), and KAGRA, which is a project of the gravitational wave studies group at the Institute for Cosmic Ray Research (ICRR) of the University of Tokyo.

A Vibration: Why Outer Space?

A Vibration is a piece I wrote about gravitational waves and how we are all connected. It even uses the actual sonifications of gravitational waves as part of the score. I knew I wanted to write about space, since this was the next logical concept in my song cycle (more on that next year). There are several reasons I thought wanted to incorporate gravitational waves into this piece about space, including:

Gravitational wave detection is brand new (to us humans), and it’s fun to (mentally) explore brand new things with our mind-pencils and mind-calipers.

The first gravitational wave detection way back in 2015 will mark the beginning of a new era of astronomy, cosmology, and astrophysics. Not convinced? Think of the telescope’s inventor, German-Dutch spectacle maker Hans Lippershey (who applied for his telescope-y seeing machine’s patent in 1608, before Galileo Galilei had heard of the contraption). At the time, Lippershey’s friends must have thought he came up with a fun toy, since it had only a 3X magnification. But this toy launched an era of astronomical discovery. Today’s telescopes are 100 million times more sensitive than that one, and can take pictures like this one that contains about 10,000 galaxies, the oldest of which we are seeing as they were 13.2 billion years ago. Side note: The telescope it was taken from (the Hubble) is not on planet Earth. Which is round. Lippershey couldn’t have known that his invention would lead to our understanding these things.

(Image source: ligo.caltech.edu)

Similarly, we can't yet envision what this new field of study will ultimately lead us to understand, but I can imagine that the next steps might lead us to things like a better understanding of the topography of spacetime (how space is shaped); multiverses (multiple universes); dark matter; dark energy; other forces, fields, and particles that interact with gravity that we haven't yet discovered; ways to harness gravity (anti-gravity perhaps?); and new theoretical ways to harness spacetime topography (like worm holes and warp drives). And if these seem far fetched, just realize that that this is probably just the beginning — you should hold onto your hat for what those discoveries will then lead to. I think our understanding in 100 years will be startling to behold to the primitive, nay, backward people of 2017, who are at the beginning of the gravitational era.

I've been curious about this since reading about Einstein's life, about his work on relativity (which interestingly he wanted to call “the theory of invariance” at various points in his life). I have been curious about other ramifications of relativity as well. It seems like gravitational waves might lead us to some of those other ramifications. In tribute to him, here is Einstein’s Field equation which he was so proud of — maybe you can see ramifications that he couldn’t, and then you can win your own prize for it:

Finally, I love that this is yet another thing in the universe that is infinitesimal and immense, which turned out to be a theme in my piece.The gravitational wave vibrations we've measured are truly infinitesimal, and yet the event that caused them was immense. Sometimes observing infinitesimal things lead to understandings of immense things if you look backwards in time, and gravitational waves are one of those infinitesimal things.

Astrophysics In the Future: More Space with LISA

This work relies on the work of many other groups and scientists, including the ones working concurrently at LISA Pathfinder, which is a project that aims to place gravitational wave detectors in space instead of from Earth.

Here’s an artist’s rendition of one of the Lisa spacecraft:

(Image source: lisa.nasa.gov)

The next major evolution of the LIGO detectors will be to place 3-4 spacecraft in orbit around our planet to make a detector. This project is called LISA (Laser Interferometer Space Antenna), because you have to spell something with your words.

The advantage of putting detectors in space include eliminating ground-based vibrations (earthquakes, storms, explosions, etc), and the ability to measure very low frequency gravity waves. Whereas Earth-based LIGO’s targets are rapidly orbiting things, LISA will be able to see slower things (like ultra-compact binaries, supermassive black hole mergers, and extreme mass ratio inspirals, among other exotic possibilities.) I can't wait to see what happens next in astronomy!

Here’s to the cool things that we haven’t yet figured out about the universe but that will blow our minds when physicists do!

First, you may want to team up with a bunch of other smart people (it often takes a big village these days), and start discovering things—really important groundbreaking things that can change our understanding and that makes us humans seem either insignificant or really clever.

Where to Look For Your Nobel Prize-Winning Ideas

Shoulder Standing

You may want to build on the work of those who preceded you. Here’s a chronological list of 15 prize-winning physics discoverers whose work seems particularly relevant today, and on whose shoulders you may want to stand on.

Max Planck: Discovery of quanta (as in, the quantum mechanics things), prize awarded in 1918

Albert Einstein: Discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect (which paved the way for practical stuff like solar panels & night vision goggles), 1921

Werner Karl Heisenberg: Creation of quantum mechanics, 1932

Chen Ning Yang and Tsung-Dao (T.D.) Lee: Discovery of violations of the principle of parity, 1957

John Bardeen, Leon Neil Cooper and John Robert Schrieffer: Developed theory of superconductivitym 1972

Leo Esaki and Ivar Giaever: Discovered tunneling in semiconductors and superconductors, 1973

Arno Allan Penzias and Robert Woodrow Wilson: Discovery of cosmic microwave background radiation, 1978

Sheldon Lee Glashow, Abdus Salam and Steven Weinberg: Investigated unified weak and electromagnetic interactions, 1979

Carlo Rubbia and Simon van der Meer: foundational research which led to discovery of the W and Z field particles, which carry the weak interaction, 1984

Martin Perl and Frederick Reines: Discovered the tau lepton and the neutrino, respectively, 1995

Jack S. Kilby: key contributor to the invention of the integrated circuit, 2000

David J. Gross, H. David Politzer and Frank Wilczek: Discovery of asymptotic freedom in strong interactions, 2004

Makoto Kobayashi and Toshihide Maskawa: Discovery of the origin of the broken symmetry which predicts the existence of at least three families of quarks, 2008.

François Englert and Peter W. Higgs: Discovery of one of the mechanisms that gives mass to subatomic particles, 2013

Takaaki Kajita and Arthur B. McDonald: discovery of neutrino oscillations, which show that neutrinos have mass, 2016

Extra credit: I’ve left a bunch out of my list. You might want to look at the rest of the 111 Nobel Physics prize awards to see if there are other shoulders you could stand on.
