Biography

Montagu Sherard Dawes Butler is Notable.

Born in 1873 at Julian Hill, a grade II listed building in Harrow, London, to Spencer Percival Butler and Mary Kendall, Butler was educated at Haileybury and Pembroke College, Cambridge. He graduated with a double first, having also been President of the Cambridge Union Society in Easter (summer) Term 1895. He became a Fellow of Pembroke in 1895 and entered the Indian Civil Service in 1896 having come top in the entrance exam.

Late in 1896 Butler went to the Punjab in India. In 1901 he married Anne Smith, daughter of George Smith. He was Settlement Officer, Kotah State, 1904-9. Deputy Commissioner, Lahore, 1909-11. He was appointed a Companion of the Order of the Indian Empire in 1909 and served as Deputy Secretary to the Government of India (Home Department) in 1911. He was joint Secretary of the Islington Royal Commission on Public Services in India from 1912-15. He then served as Deputy Commissioner, Attock District, Punjab from 1915-19 before being made President of the Punjab Legislative Council from 1821 to 1822; he was Secretary to the Government of India Education, Health and Lands Dept., 1922-4; President of the Council of State of India 1924-1925; Governor of the Central Provinces, 1925-33. Chancellor of Nagpur University, 1925-33. [1]

During his service in India he received a series of commendations for honourable service: a Companion of the Order of the Indian Empire in 1909; a Commander of the Royal Victorian Order in 1911; a Companion of the Order of the Bath in 1916, a Commander of the Order of the British Empire on 30 December 1919; he was appointed a Knight Bachelor in the 1924 King's Birthday Honours, and was further knighted as a Knight Commander of the Order of the Star of India in December of that year.[2]

Upon leaving India in 1933, he was made Lieutenant-Governor of the Isle of Man, 1933-7. He was elected Master of Pembroke College, Cambridge, in 1937 and served two full terms. He also served on the borough council, the council of the senate and the financial board of the university. He was elected as mayor of Cambridge in 1941 and served two terms. He retired in 1948.[3] He died in 1952.
